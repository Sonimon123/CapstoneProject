Board Game Data Analysis

An analysis of data from BoardGameGeek.com covering over 15000 games

A project by JR Kargbo

Introduction

This is a webpage describing an analysis of some data collected from BoardGameGeek.com. Board Game Geek is the internet's largest database on board games, with information on tens of thousands of titles. The site also has a large community of users, who mainly consist of a group of fans of more niche, modern, hobbyist games rather than more traditional classic games. Users use the site to rate games, log their collections, and discuss their thoughts, opinions, and strategies with others. This project is an attempt to see how much different aspects of a game can relate to it's popularity amongst the site's users. It does this by looking at 3 dependant variables: the game's rank, the game's average rating, and the amount of people who own the game. We find out how much of a correlation there is between these dependant variables and many different factors, such as the mechanics a game uses, its complexity and it's player count. Finally, on the 5 variables with the most positive and negative correlation, we drill deeper in on the relationship with a multivariable linear regression. Dataset Info

While there are tens of thousands of games on the site, many of them are not ranked due to a low number of votes. These are usually titles that either haven't been released yet or are so obscure that most member's won't care about them. As such, as a way to reasonably limit the dataset, as well as to help improve the quality of the data, I've decided to only track ranked games. Since this still leaves us with over 17000 games being analyzed, I considered this to be more than enough to gain a good picture of the site. In addition, we're not looking at expansions that require the original game, only standalone releases.

To explain our dependant variables, the Ranking of a game is controlled mainly by it's Geek Rating (a variable we're not tracking). The precice way the Rank and Geek Rating are determined are not 100 percent known (in order to prevent people from gaming the system), but what we do know is that a Bayesian Average, where we add a good amount of dummy ratings to the game's score, skewing most games closer to a baseline level. This means that games require more ratings to increase it's Rank and Geek Rating compared to its average score. Thankfully, the average rating and the number of owners are much simpler variables: The average rating is simply an average of all of the ratings users have given a game (each game can only be rated by a user once), and the amount of owners are the amount of people on the site who have logged that they currently own the game.
